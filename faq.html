GLP-1 medications

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a naturally occurring hormone that regulates appetite and blood sugar. GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide and tirzepatide mimic this hormone, signaling to your brain that you're full, slowing gastric emptying, and reducing cravings. They are FDA-approved for weight management and have been studied extensively in large-scale clinical trials.

Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that reduces appetite and supports sustained weight loss — the active ingredient in Ozempic and Wegovy. Tirzepatide is a dual GIP + GLP-1 receptor agonist (the active ingredient in Mounjaro and Zepbound) that targets two appetite-regulating pathways simultaneously. Clinical trials show tirzepatide produces up to 22% body weight reduction on average, compared to approximately 15% with semaglutide. Your clinician will recommend the right option based on your health profile and goals.

Compounded medications contain the same active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) as brand-name drugs, but are prepared by a licensed 503B FDA-registered compounding pharmacy. Compounded versions are not FDA-approved but use the same active compound. They are typically more affordable and allow for custom dosing tailored to your body. Rylo Health only works with accredited, inspected pharmacies.

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every cell of your body that plays a critical role in energy production, DNA repair, and cellular aging. NAD+ levels decline with age. Our peptide therapy program — which includes NAD+, glutathione, and sermorelin — is designed for those focused on longevity, anti-aging, and peak cellular performance. It's ideal as a standalone or complement to GLP-1 therapy.

Safety & side effects

GLP-1 receptor agonists have been studied in clinical trials involving tens of thousands of patients and are considered safe for most adults when prescribed by a licensed clinician. As with any medication, there are risks and contraindications. Rylo Health clinicians conduct a thorough medical review before prescribing and monitor your progress throughout treatment. Not everyone is a candidate — your clinician will determine eligibility based on your health history.

The most commonly reported side effects are gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, and reduced appetite. These are typically mild to moderate and tend to improve as your body adjusts, especially when starting at a low dose and titrating gradually. Less common but serious side effects include pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, and — in rare cases — thyroid tumors. Your clinician will review your personal risk profile before prescribing.

GLP-1 medications may not be appropriate if you have a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2, a history of pancreatitis, severe gastrointestinal disease, are pregnant or breastfeeding, or are under 18. Certain medications may also interact. Your clinician will review your full history during the intake process to determine eligibility.

GLP-1 receptor agonists were originally developed for type 2 diabetes management and have a well-established safety profile in that context. However, combining them with other diabetes medications (especially insulin or sulfonylureas) requires careful dose management to avoid hypoglycemia. Always disclose all existing medications and conditions in your intake form. Your Rylo clinician will coordinate your care accordingly.

Results & timelines

Results vary based on starting weight, adherence, diet, and activity. Clinical trials show semaglutide produces an average of ~15% body weight reduction over 68 weeks, while tirzepatide produces up to ~22% over 72 weeks. In practice, members commonly report 1–3 lbs of loss per week early in treatment, tapering to a slower, steady pace over time. Your Rylo clinician will set realistic expectations based on your specific profile.

Most members notice reduced appetite and early weight changes within the first 2–4 weeks. Meaningful, visible results typically appear by weeks 8–12. GLP-1 therapy is a long-term intervention — the biggest results are seen at the 3-, 6-, and 12-month marks as doses are titrated upward and your body adapts to treatment.

Clinical research shows that many people regain weight after stopping GLP-1 therapy, because the medication is actively suppressing appetite signals that return when treatment ends. This is why Rylo Health focuses on concurrent lifestyle support alongside medication. Your clinician will discuss a long-term strategy — including when and how to appropriately taper — as part of your ongoing care plan.
